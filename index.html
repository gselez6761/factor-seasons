Errors caught and corrected by the team
Compound vs. summed returns Decade chart originally used arithmetic sums of monthly returns. Compound returns showed Momentum lost 19% in the 2000s — not gained 10% as summed. April 2009's −34% single-month crash erased years of small gains.
"The order flips" AI claimed all six factors invert ranking between calm and fearful months. Only Market and Profitability actually swap; the other four are roughly stationary. Deck rewritten: "a story about two factors trading places."
VIX sample period AI's deck said "every month since 1963." The VIX wasn't published until 1990. Corrected to "every month from 1990 through 2025."
Long-short portfolio framing Finding 1 deck called the chart "growth of $1 above the risk-free rate." That's only true for Mkt-RF — the other five are long-short academic portfolios. Deck rewritten with a caveat on real-world underperformance.
CMA tooltip mechanism AI explained Investment factor as firms "hoarding cash." CMA is constructed from asset-growth rates, not cash reserves. Replaced with the actual mechanism.
Value drought dates AI claimed "seven straight years of negative rolling 5-year HML returns" from 2013–2021. The series doesn't go negative that broadly; copy softened to "drought era" without overclaiming the window.
"Every rally faded" (ZIRP tooltip) Written in a rewrite and caught by the team before committing. The 2009–2015 bull market was a sustained rally; the correct observation was that factor leadership rotated, not that rallies reversed.
Stat band clutter AI added summary stat boxes above the heatmap and portfolio grid. Team rejected them as "metric-dashboardy." Removed; the most important number was folded into the deck instead.

When the news says "the market was up today," they mean one number. But researchers have identified systematic patterns inside that number - groups of stocks that consistently behave differently from each other, decade after decade. The six most studied are called factors. Some have rewarded investors for sixty years. None of them work all the time.

Four findings from sixty-two years of data. Which factor wins changes by decade, by regime, and by how scared the market is. The charts below show how.

Data sources

  • Ken French Data Library - monthly returns for all six factors, July 1963 through December 2025 (752 months)
  • FRED / NBER - recession indicator (USREC): 1 during recession, 0 during expansion, covering the full sample
  • CBOE VIX - monthly volatility index ("fear gauge"), available January 1990 onward

What is a factor?

A factor is a measurable trait of a company - its size, how cheaply it trades, how profitable it is - that has historically been associated with higher returns. Researchers build a factor by buying companies that score high on the trait and shorting (betting against) companies that score low. The six factors below are among the most widely studied in academic finance.

01
Finding One · The Sixty-Year Race
The Same Dollar. Six Different Rules. Wildly Different Outcomes.
Each line shows the historical premium earned by following one strategy - the extra return above a simple baseline. For the Market line, the baseline is cash (T-bills). For the other five, the baseline is the opposite side of the bet: small stocks vs. big stocks, cheap vs. expensive, profitable vs. unprofitable, and so on. We strip out the baseline so every line measures the same thing: how much each rule paid you for taking on its specific risk.

Momentum and the Market run neck-and-neck for decades, until Momentum's vertical drop in 2009. Size, the first factor ever documented in academic finance, barely triples in sixty years.

Finding Two · Recessions vs. Expansions
During Recessions, the Market Loses - But Defensive Factors Hold.
Using the NBER recession indicator, we split every month from 1963 through 2025 into recession (85 months) and expansion (667 months). The Market is the clear loser in downturns. The Investment factor (CMA) is the standout winner - conservative firms that avoided aggressive expansion suffer the least when the cycle turns. Momentum nearly disappears in recessions: there are no clean trends to follow when the economy is lurching.

Finding Two (cont.) · Market vs. Quality
When Investors Are Afraid, the Market Has Averaged the Weakest Returns.
We use the VIX - a real-time index of expected stock market volatility, often called the "fear gauge" - as a proxy for investor anxiety. We split every month from 1990 through 2025 into "calm" (VIX below its long-run median of 17.5) and "fearful" (VIX above). In fearful months, the Market loses on average and "boring" Profitability wins handily. The other four factors barely move - so this is really a story about two factors trading places.

Finding Four · The Rotation
Every Decade Has a Different Winner - Including the One Where the Market Lost.
The chart below shows each factor's compounded return for each decade - what a $1 investment at the start of the decade would have grown to. Momentum led the 70s, 80s, and 90s. Then in the 2000s - the dot-com bust and Financial Crisis decade - the Market itself lost 27%, while the Profitability factor gained 108%. A 135-percentage-point gap between two strategies, for an entire ten-year stretch.

A Closing Thought

There is no permanent winner. The practical response is diversifying across factors.

If the past sixty years prove anything, it's that no single rule - not "buy the index," not "buy small caps," not "follow the trend" - works across every economic season. The Market led the 2010s. Profitability quietly won the 2000s. Momentum lost five years of gains in eight months. The size premium has weakened significantly in recent decades.

What the data does support is owning a portfolio exposed to several factors at once. The factors are imperfectly correlated (only 13 months in 752 had them all moving together). When one is having a bad decade, another has historically helped offset the loss. This is the logic behind smart beta: index funds and ETFs that systematically tilt toward one or more factors instead of simply weighting stocks by market size, letting investors capture factor premiums at low cost without an active manager.

But the rotation documented here also underlies a more active approach. Many portfolio managers use factor exposure as a lever: overweighting profitability and quality when fear is elevated, rotating toward value when inflation is rising, pulling back on momentum when a trend looks exhausted. The regimes in this article are exactly the signals those managers watch. The difference between smart beta and active factor investing is not what you own, but whether you tilt your holdings based on a view of where the economy is headed.
