The Hidden Patterns That Decide Whether a Stock Beats the Market

For sixty years, the same handful of forces have quietly shaped equity returns — and they switch on and off with the seasons of the economy.

By Gregory Seleznev, Martin Ye, and Alex Dong University of Maryland 12 min read

Every investor knows the market goes up and down. Far fewer know that why it goes up and down — and which kinds of stocks lead the charge — depends almost entirely on where the economy is in its cycle. Researchers have spent six decades documenting this pattern. They call the underlying drivers factors.

A factor is just a measurable trait of a company — its size, how cheaply it trades, how profitable it is — that has, over long stretches of history, predicted future returns. Some factors thrive during recessions. Others vanish for a decade and then come roaring back. The story below walks through six of the most important ones, what they mean, and why they keep changing seasons.

What, exactly, is a “factor”?

A factor is a stock-picking rule that has, repeatedly across decades and across countries, separated winners from losers. The rule is mechanical: rank every stock by some measurable trait — size, price-to-book ratio, profitability — then compare how the top group performs versus the bottom group. If the top group consistently beats the bottom group, the trait is said to be a “priced factor.”

Six factors dominate modern academic finance. Each one captures a different reason a stock might be expected to earn higher returns: as compensation for risk, as a reward for patience, or because most investors over- or under-react to a particular signal.

An analogy. Think of factors like the wind, currents, and tide that affect a sailboat. The boat is your portfolio; the wind is the overall market. But currents (size), waves (value), and tide (profitability) all push the boat too — and whether they help or hinder depends on the season.
Chapter I · Six Factors

Six lenses on the same market

Since the 1960s, researchers have isolated six characteristics of stocks that consistently predict future returns. Each one is a different way of asking why one stock beats another.

The next six chapters take them one at a time. We'll plot each factor's rolling 5-year return — a smoothed measure of how it has actually paid investors across the full 1963–2025 sample.

Each factor is a hypothesis about human behavior or economic risk. The chart on the right is the test.
Chapter II · The Market

The premium you pay rent on

The market premium (Mkt-RF) is the most fundamental of all factors: the extra return from owning stocks instead of risk-free Treasury bills. Every investor in equities bears this risk the moment they buy in.

The rolling 5-year window has gone negative twice in our sample — after the dot-com bust and after the 2008 Financial Crisis — and recovered both times. Stretch the window to 15 years and it has never been negative.

Stocks pay roughly 7–8% a year over T-bills, on average. You earn it by enduring the years when they don't.

Chapter III · The Size Effect

Small caps used to win. They don't anymore.

Small companies — measured by market capitalization — were once a near-guaranteed source of excess return. The factor is called SMB: Small Minus Big. Smaller firms are harder to research, less liquid, and risk-laden, so investors historically demanded a premium to hold them.

Look at the rolling 5-year return after 2014. It turns persistently negative. The premium has, by most accounts, disappeared. Possible culprits: passive indexing, broader analyst coverage, and (uncomfortably) the chance that the original 1980s research was overfitted to noise.

Just because a factor worked in the textbooks doesn't mean it still works in the wild. SMB is the cautionary tale.
Chapter IV · Value

The factor that tests your patience

Value (HML — High Minus Low book-to-market) is the most argued-about factor in all of finance. The thesis: investors systematically overpay for exciting growth companies, which leaves boring, cheap companies systematically underpriced. Buying the unloved and waiting is the strategy.

The rolling line shows decade-long cycles. After a strong 1970s and 80s, value entered a brutal drought from 2013 to 2021 — before mounting its biggest comeback in the entire 60-year sample after COVID-19.

Value's cycle is measured in years, not months. Most investors abandon it just before it rebounds.

Chapter V · Profitability

Quality is quietly consistent

Profitability — RMW, Robust Minus Weak — joined the Fama-French model as recently as 2015. The insight is intuitive: financially strong, high-margin companies tend to beat low-margin ones, even after you adjust for size and value.

The rolling 5-year line spends almost no time below zero. It is the steadiest factor in the set. When uncertainty spikes, investors flee toward profitable balance sheets — which is precisely when this factor pays off.

Profitability behaves like quality insurance: it underperforms in late-cycle euphoria and rewards you exactly when you need it most.
Chapter VI · Investment

Capital discipline beats empire-building

The investment factor (CMA — Conservative Minus Aggressive) captures a quieter truth: firms that grow assets slowly tend to outperform firms that grow them aggressively. Empire-builders overpay for acquisitions and overestimate their future returns.

CMA's worst stretch coincides with the dot-com boom, when the most aggressively investing technology firms briefly ran the table — before all of it reversed in the 2000–03 collapse.

CMA is negatively correlated with momentum: firms with explosive recent growth often have explosive recent capital spending. CMA is, in effect, a natural hedge against momentum risk.
Chapter VII · Momentum

Pennies in front of a steamroller

Momentum (Mom) had the highest average return of any factor for forty years. The mechanism: investors update their beliefs slowly, so recent winners keep winning — right up until the trend snaps.

Watch the 1990s. Then watch March 2009. As the Financial Crisis flipped, momentum's short positions — the beaten-down stocks the strategy was betting against — exploded upward. The factor lost 83% in six months, dragging the rolling window underwater for years.

Extraordinarily consistent — until the one catastrophic reversal that wipes out years of gains in weeks.

Chapter VIII · The Cycle

Six factors, six panels, the same recessions

To compare honestly, we split the chart into six small panels — one per factor — all sharing the same time axis and the same y-scale. The gray bands are NBER recessions, marked identically on every panel.

Now the comparison is clean. Notice how the Market line dips after recessions, while Profitability and Investment barely flinch. Momentum's collapse in 2009 is visible as a single deep trough — distinct from anything the other factors do.

Each factor reacts to the cycle in its own way. The economy doesn't lift or sink them all together — and that is precisely why investors care about owning more than one.
Chapter IX · Each Recession in Turn

Not every downturn is the same

The 1973–75 oil shock hit factors in a profoundly different way than the 2020 COVID crash, which lasted just two months. Each cell to the right shows the total factor return accumulated during a single recession.

The 2007–09 Financial Crisis, highlighted, stands apart: deepest market loss, a momentum implosion, and a value collapse — all happening at once.

Profitability and Investment are the only factors that posted positive returns in a majority of recessions. That is why they form the spine of most defensive allocations.
Chapter X · Value's Lost Decade

Seven years of nothing — then a flood

The shaded region marks 2013–2021 — value's long drought era. The rolling 5-year window dipped underwater for several years, and by 2019 many of the largest institutional investors in the world had formally retired their value strategies.

Then came COVID-19. Cheap stocks became extraordinarily cheap. And value posted its largest 3-year recovery in the entire 60-year sample.

Mean-reversion is real, but the timing is brutal. The rolling window was negative for nearly five years before the rebound — most investors couldn't hold through it.
Chapter XI · The Momentum Crash

How a “winning” strategy lost five years in six months

Strong for decades. Then, in early 2009, the strategy's short positions — the beaten-down stocks that had fallen the most — snapped back violently. The result: −83% in six months, a sudden reversal that took the rolling window five full years to dig out of.

Momentum crashes always coincide with the same conditions: a sharp market reversal after a prolonged decline. Which is to say, exactly when investor stress is at its peak and drawdowns are hardest to hold.

Momentum's reward looks consistent in the average — but the average hides one or two episodes that can erase a decade of gains.
Chapter XII · Recession vs. Expansion

The same factor — two different worlds

Every month since 1963, classified as either expansion or recession by the National Bureau of Economic Research. The bars to the right show average monthly return for each factor in each regime.

The market premium turns negative on average during recessions — almost tautologically. But Profitability and Investment hold up consistently across all eight downturns in the sample.

Chapter XIII · Rising vs. Falling Rates

Value lives and dies by interest rates

Each month is classified by whether the Federal Funds Rate trended up, down, or sideways over the prior three months. Value stocks are particularly rate-sensitive: their valuations depend on long-duration cash flows.

Value (HML) earns its highest returns when rates are falling, and bleeds when they rise — a pattern that shaped the brutal 2022 rate-hike cycle and explains the recent value resurgence as the Fed paused.

Chapter XIV · Fear and Volatility

What happens when the market is afraid

The VIX is Wall Street's fear gauge. We split each month by whether the VIX was above or below 17.49 — the median value across our entire sample, not a magic threshold. Even by that simple cut, factor behavior shifts dramatically.

In fearful markets, Profitability holds up best. Momentum, which extrapolates recent trends, becomes especially fragile: a sudden reversal can flip the long-short book inside-out overnight.
