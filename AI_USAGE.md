# AI Usage Documentation

**Project:** Factor Seasons (CMSC 471 Final Project, Spring 2026, Team 10)
**Tool used:** Anthropic's Claude Code (CLI), with Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.7 models
**Plugin used:** `frontend-design` (Anthropic plugin, for the WSJ aesthetic redesign)
**Files in scope:** `index.html`, `insights.html`, `data/*` (analysis only - data files were
generated by the team's Python pipeline)

---

## Summary

Claude Code was used as a coding assistant and a writing/editing assistant. It was *not*
used as an oracle: every quantitative claim it produced was verified against the source
data, every visualization design was reviewed and revised, and several of its proposed
framings were rejected outright. The team treated AI output the same way a senior engineer
treats a junior engineer's PR - useful, but reviewed line-by-line.

This document records what AI did, what humans did, where AI was wrong, and how those
errors were caught and fixed.

---

## What AI did

### 1. Initial scaffolding (`index.html` - the original scrollytelling page)
- Wrote the IntersectionObserver-based scroll-step manager.
- Wrote the D3 rendering code for the rolling 5-year chart, the recession scorecard heatmap,
  the regime bar charts, the annual heatmap, and the 25-portfolio explorer.
- Wrote initial copy (deck, step cards, callouts) which the team reviewed and rewrote in
  several places.

### 2. WSJ editorial redesign (current `index.html` aesthetic)
The dark, neon-accented original theme was replaced with a Wall Street Journal–style
editorial aesthetic. AI was given an explicit design brief (cream background, serif
headlines, muted editorial factor palette, WSJ red accent, thin double-rule section breaks)
and produced the CSS overhaul. The team reviewed and approved the result.

### 3. Companion `insights.html` page
A second page was built when the team decided the original 14-step scrollytelling was too
broad for the target audience. AI was instructed to:
- Research **documented**, accessible, peer-reviewed factor phenomena.
- Run statistical analyses on the team's data files (Python in a sandboxed shell).
- Cross-reference with finance literature.
- Propose a tight set of 4 findings.

The proposals were debated. Several initial ideas were rejected by the team for being
either too academic ("post-recession recovery phases") or insufficiently surprising
("factors disagree wildly"). The final four were selected together: cumulative growth,
annual disagreement, decade rotation, and the calm-vs-fear comparison.

### 4. CFA-style validation pass
The team prompted Claude to "look through this page with the scrutiny of a CFA or PM and
find anything wrong." Claude ran additional Python checks against the source data and
flagged five real issues - the most important being that the original decade chart used
**arithmetic-summed** monthly returns rather than **compound** returns, which materially
distorted several headline numbers (Momentum in the 2000s went from "+10% summed" to
"−19% compound"). All five fixes were applied.

### 5. Documentation
This file and `INSIGHTS.md` were written by AI under team direction.

---

## What AI did *not* do

- **Did not produce the data.** All 11 JSON files in `data/` were generated by a Python
  pipeline written by the team that downloaded from Ken French's data library, FRED, and
  Yahoo Finance, then merged and labeled regimes.
- **Did not select the topic** or design the project's pedagogical arc. The team decided
  what story to tell.
- **Did not run unattended.** Every commit was reviewed; AI does not have push access.
- **Did not invent data.** Every number on the page was verified against the source JSONs
  or computed by Python from them. Where Claude initially wrote uncited claims (e.g., "value
  rebounds first after recessions"), they were checked against the actual data - and in
  several cases the AI's initial framing turned out to be subtly wrong (see corrections
  section below).

## Specific examples of corrections made

These are real moments where AI output was wrong or misleading and the team caught it.
They are documented here in the spirit of academic honesty about AI tooling.

### Correction 1 - Date inconsistency in the value-drought annotation
Initial AI-written copy on `index.html` (step 9) claimed "seven straight years of negative
rolling 5-year returns for the value factor" with a shade band from 2013–2021. The team
flagged that the rolling 5-year HML series doesn't actually go negative until ~2014–2017,
not the full 2013–2021 span. AI rewrote the copy to refer to the broader "drought era"
without overclaiming the rolling-window negativity duration.

### Correction 2 - JavaScript bug from automatic semicolon insertion
When AI added a small-multiples view to step 7, the bottom rendering broke silently. The
team noticed the panels weren't drawing. The cause was an `[-20, 20].forEach(...)` line
following a chained `.attr(...)` call without a leading semicolon - JavaScript's ASI
parsed the bracket as array-index access on the d3 selection, returning `undefined`, and
the subsequent `.forEach` threw a `TypeError`. AI fixed by inserting a leading semicolon.
Lesson: AI-generated code is only as correct as your review process.

### Correction 3 - Stat band clutter
AI added 4-cell summary stat boxes ("Years of data: 62 · Years where range > 30 pp: 35 ·
…") above the annual heatmap and the 25-portfolio grid as a way to lead with computed
numbers. The team rejected this for being visually noisy and "metric-dashboardy." AI
removed both stat bands and folded the most important number into the deck instead.

### Correction 4 - Misleading framing of "the fear flip"
AI's initial Finding 4 deck claimed the "order of factors inverts" between calm and
fearful regimes. Closer inspection showed only **two** factors actually swap places
(Market and Profitability); the other four are roughly stationary. The team asked Claude
to be honest about this, and the deck was rewritten to "this is really a story about *two*
factors trading places." The center caption was changed from "THE FLIP" to "Market ⇄ Quality."

### Correction 5 - Compound vs. summed returns in the decade chart
The decade-by-decade bar chart originally used arithmetic sums of monthly returns. For
high-return decade-factor pairs the gap to compound returns is large (1990s Mom: summed
+134% vs. compound +257%). Most damagingly, the chart claimed Momentum was a small *winner*
in the 2000s (+10% summed) when in compound terms it was a clear *loser* (−19%) - because
April 2009's −34% wiped out years of small monthly gains. The team flagged this, AI
switched the calculation to compound, and the deck was rewritten with the corrected
numbers (the 2000s "Market lost / Quality won" gap grew from 100 pp to 135 pp).

### Correction 6 - Folkloric mechanism in CMA hover tooltip
AI's initial explanation for why the Investment factor (CMA) holds up in fearful markets
said "conservative firms hoarded cash during the boom, which became a moat in fear." This
is folkloric - CMA is constructed from firms' asset-growth rates, not their cash reserves.
The team replaced it with the actual mechanism: slow asset growth → less exposure to
overpriced acquisitions and capex that get repriced when fear hits.

### Correction 7 - Sample-period mismatch in Finding 4
AI's initial deck said "every month since 1963" for the calm-vs-fear comparison. The VIX
wasn't published until 1990. The deck was corrected to "every month from 1990 through 2025."

### Correction 8 - "$1 invested above the risk-free rate"
The Finding 1 deck initially called the cumulative growth chart "growth of $1 invested
above the risk-free rate." That's only true for the Mkt-RF series - the other five factors
are long-short academic portfolios, not "above risk-free rate" investments. The deck was
rewritten to clarify these are "academic long-short factor portfolios" with a footer
warning that real-world factor ETFs underperform academic returns by 0.5–2%/year.

### Correction 9 - Incorrect default visualization for Chapter VIII
The original chart for "When the Economy Contracts" was a single line chart with all six
factors plotted simultaneously, plus recession bands. The team noted it was visually
cluttered. AI proposed and implemented small multiples (six mini-panels, one per factor,
shared scales) - a much cleaner read.

## Honest assessment

Claude Code accelerated implementation by an estimated 5–10× compared to writing all
HTML/CSS/D3.js from scratch. The team's role was:

1. **Direction.** Deciding what to build, what to cut, what story to tell.
2. **Data integrity.** Producing and verifying the source data; running independent Python
   audits when AI-produced numbers needed checking.
3. **Editorial judgment.** Rewriting copy that was technically correct but framed misleadingly,
   rejecting framings that overclaimed.
4. **Visual judgment.** Approving or rejecting design choices; pushing back when AI produced
   "metric-dashboardy" clutter.
5. **Domain skepticism.** Catching folkloric mechanism explanations (the CMA "cash hoarding"
   one); insisting on peer-reviewed citations rather than plausible-sounding stories.

The most consistent failure mode of AI on this project was **overclaiming**: writing decks
that were sharper than the data warranted (e.g., "the order of factors flips" when only two
flip, "seven years of negative returns" when it was closer to four). These were caught by
the team rereading the output against the data. We do not assume future readers will catch
similar issues automatically, which is why the page now includes inline caveats (academic
vs. real-world returns, sample-period notes, and the explicit "this is really a story about
two factors" qualifier).

## Session 2 - May 2026 polish and editorial pass

This session was a sustained back-and-forth between the primary author (Gregory) and Claude
Code focused on copy quality, chart UX, and removing the "AI-written" feel from the page.
The session did not add new visualizations or data - it refined what existed.

### What AI did in this session

**Copy editing (human-directed):**
- Replaced all em-dashes with hyphens site-wide after the author flagged the prose sounded
  AI-generated. ("I don't want it to read like an AI-generated article.")
- Rewrote the intro paragraph twice - first to be more CFA-defensible ("systematic patterns
  inside that number"), then again after the author rejected a specific sentence as "not
  seeming like a very true statement."
- Updated the page title and lede paragraph under author direction.
- Rewrote the ZIRP tooltip after the author caught that "every rally faded" was factually
  wrong - rallies in 2009-2015 *persisted*, it was cross-factor leadership that rotated.
- Added an inline VIX definition after the author noted readers wouldn't know what it means.

**CFA-level copy audit:**
The author asked Claude to scan all body paragraphs for overgeneralizations "with the
knowledge of a CFA." Nine issues were identified and fixed:
- "Predicts future stock returns" softened to "historically been associated with higher returns"
- "Academic finance has converged on" changed to "among the most widely studied"
- "Dominated" softened to "led" (re: Momentum in the 70s–90s)
- Finding 4 headline changed from "becomes the worst factor" to "has averaged the weakest returns"
- "The data says the opposite" reframed to apply only to high-fear months, not broadly
- "Size premium has all but disappeared" softened to "weakened significantly" (the Asness
  et al. 2018 result shows it persists when controlling for quality)
- "Usually compensating" softened to "has often helped offset"
- HML card rewritten: removed "waiting for mean-reversion" (one-sided theory) and replaced
  with acknowledgment that the mechanism is still debated
- Stale reference to the leader strip (already deleted) removed from Finding 2 deck

**Finance Q&A:**
The author asked several finance questions during the session - why Value does poorly in
crises, why Profitability and Value both fell in 1999, and what ZIRP means in the tooltip
context. These were answered and where relevant the answers were used to improve the copy.
The author also asked whether a CFA would agree with a proposed sentence, leading to a
revision before the change was committed.

**Visualization changes (human-directed):**
- Heatmap: removed numbers inside cells, tightened color scale domain for more contrast,
  removed the "Year's leader" strip, fixed SVG overflow (switched to viewBox), restored key
  year timeline annotations with plainer typography, added a red-to-green gradient legend,
  added All/Winners/Losers toggle buttons, added 1973 "Oil embargo / stagflation" annotation.
- Chart 1 (cumulative): replaced clipping end-of-line labels with a clickable legend in the
  top-left corner. Clicking a factor isolates it (dims others to 7% opacity). Hovering over
  the legend blocks the crosshair tooltip from firing. Dollar values removed from legend
  after author found them confusing.
- Chart 2 (VIX flip): negative bar value labels moved to right of zero line to stop clipping
  with factor names; factor names moved to a fixed left margin outside the chart area.
- Chart 4 (decades): bars made taller (barH 9 → 14), gap between decade labels and chart
  area increased, 2000s highlight band removed at author's request.
- Axis labels added to all charts that were missing them.
- Visualization order changed: VIX flip moved from Finding 4 to Finding 2 after the author
  agreed it was the strongest analytical result and was being buried at the end.
- `insights.html` deleted at author's request (consolidated into `index.html`).
- Various UI labels removed: "Market ⇄ Quality" center caption, top navigation bar,
  double-rule border on section headers.

### What the human directed in this session

Every change in this session was initiated by the author. Examples of human judgment calls:
- Rejecting the first CFA-audit rewrite of a sentence as "not a very true statement"
- Catching that "every rally faded" was factually wrong before it was committed
- Deciding the VIX flip was the strongest finding and should move earlier
- Rejecting the first legend design as "ugly and not intuitive" - the dollar values were
  removed entirely after the author asked "why does it have numbers in it?"
- Asking follow-up finance questions ("why did Value do badly in financial crises?") and
  using the answers to evaluate whether the tooltip copy was correct
- Choosing to delete insights.html rather than maintain two files

### What AI got wrong in this session

- **"Every rally faded"** - written in the ZIRP tooltip rewrite, immediately caught by the
  author. The 2009-2015 bull market was a sustained rally; the correct observation was that
  factor *leadership* rotated, not that rallies reversed. Fixed before committing.
- **Legend with dollar values** - the initial legend redesign included final dollar values
  per factor. The author rejected this as confusing. The second version removed them entirely.
- **`maxAbs` initialization error** - the color scale legend was inserted before the `maxAbs`
  variable was defined, causing a `ReferenceError` at runtime. Caught immediately from the
  browser console and fixed by reordering the declarations.

## What we learned about working with AI on a graded project

- **AI is best at structure, worst at framing.** It produces correct charts and code quickly;
  it produces *plausible* prose that needs editorial review. The plausible prose is the
  hazard.
- **Verification is non-optional.** Every number must be re-derived from source. We caught
  the compound-vs-summed mistake only because we ran an audit pass - without it, that
  mistake would have shipped.
- **AI overclaims by default.** When asked "is this insight valid?", AI is biased toward
  "yes" unless you set up the prompt to actively look for problems. The CFA-audit prompt
  ("look through this with the scrutiny of a portfolio manager") was significantly more
  productive than "is anything wrong with this page?"
- **The compliance reviewer agents were genuinely useful.** Running `proposal-compliance-reviewer`
  and `fp-compliance-checker` after each major change caught coverage gaps the team
  wouldn't have noticed.
- **Human review is the bottleneck, not AI generation.** The slow part of this project was
  reading and validating; the fast part was production. Future iterations should focus
  budget on the validation pass.
